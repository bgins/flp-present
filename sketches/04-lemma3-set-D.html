§ 3::lemma 2 · bivalent initial

P has a bivalent initial configuration.

[ paper §3 ]
§ 3::lemma 3 · preservation

Let C be a bivalent configuration of P, and let e = (p, m) be an event that is applicable to C. Let 𝒞 be the set of configurations reachable from C without applying e, and let 𝒟 = e(𝒞) = {e(E) | E ∈ 𝒞 and e is applicable to E}. Then, 𝒟 contains a bivalent configuration.

[ paper §3 ]
— Aspnes: "if the adversary can keep the protocol in a bivalent configuration forever, it can prevent the processes from ever deciding."

//definitions

C — bivalent config

e = (p, m), applicable to C

𝒞 — configs reachable from C without applying e

𝒟 = e(𝒞)

//claim
𝒟 contains a bivalent configuration.
//why
assume not. Then every D ∈ 𝒟 is univalent, with both 0-valent and 1-valent members. Pick neighbors C₀, C₁ ∈ 𝒞 whose images differ in valency — Lemma 1 commutativity (Case 1) or a deciding-run argument (Case 2) gives a contradiction.
//consequence
the adversary can defer e through a schedule that lands in the bivalent member of 𝒟 — and the system stays undecided.
